Project Overview
Summary
Background
36% of UK young adults face financial fragility due to a "Financial Habit Gap" formed during their teenage years, where classroom/parental instruction fails to translate into practical financial actions.
Goal
To close this gap by transforming passive financial knowledge into proactive, daily routines during the critical 14–16 young age.
Solution
A Financial Navigator that systemizes autonomy through flexible budgeting and frictionless parental guidance, secured by a human-in-the-loop AI mentorship model.

Usability Testings
First Round · Wireframe Stage
2 major changes to the core functions after wireframes stage testing with teens.
AimTo explore ideas for additional features and test whether the current functions were understandable and usable for teens.
MethodThink aloud method via live zoom call.
DurationEach session lasted around 30–45 minutes.
Who3 teenagers from the interview pool.
Finding 01

Early tasks needed to feel more relevant for teens

Result1/3 participants questioned why they needed to set a goal, and 3/3 were confused by the "study habit" option during the goal-setting flow.
InsightSome tasks in the early experience did not feel immediately meaningful to teens. These findings suggested that early tasks should feel clearly tied to teens' real financial journey, rather than appearing as extra steps or unrelated options.
Design responseReframed goal-setting to connect it more clearly to everyday teen spending and saving habits, and removed the "study habit" option from the goal-setting flow.
UT Excel / Participant Data
Hover to zoom in
Finding 02

The investment flow did not make its learning-first intention clear before action

Result3/3 participants tried to purchase stocks directly without going through the intended discussion flow with their parents.
InsightThe stock feature was designed to help teens build a habit of thinking and talking through what to buy before taking action. However, that intention was not clear enough in the flow, so participants moved straight toward purchase instead of recognizing discussion as a core part of the experience. This suggested that investment features should communicate reflection, learning, and support more explicitly before any action.
Design responseRedesigned the stock journey to make its learning-first structure more visible, with discussion, reflection, and guided next steps clearly introduced before any investment-related action.

Finding 02 Image
Second Round · Wireframe Stage
Parents side UT showed that they wanted to support teen financial habits, but not as experts, judges, or comparison targets.
AimTo explore ideas for additional features and test whether the current functions were understandable and usable for parents with 14–16 year old children.
MethodThink out loud via zoom call.
DurationEach session lasted around 15–20 minutes.
WhoParents from first UT.
Finding 01

Parents may not be ready for having instructional responsibility in investment-related moments.

Result1/3 parents explicitly expressed concern about the chat system before confirming an investment, while the others mainly described talking to their children about basic topics like saving rather than investment decisions.
InsightParents seemed more comfortable supporting everyday money habits than leading in-depth conversations about investing. This suggested that the product should not rely on parents as the primary source of stock-related guidance, especially when accuracy and age-appropriateness felt uncertain.
Design responseInstead of relying on parents to lead stock-related conversations, I introduced AI as a first-line guide that provides accessible, age-appropriate explanations before parent involvement.
UT Excel / Participant Data
Finding 02

Parents feel encouragement should be more promoted instead of discouragement

Result2/3 questioned the "sad" icon for child's transaction history.
InsightFor teens aged 14–16, the experience should support confidence and gradual habit formation, not guilt around everyday spending.
Design responseShifted the tone of the transaction history from discouraging overspending to reinforcing small positive money habits.
Finding 03

Parents felt that direct spending comparison was not a meaningful way to support teens

Result2/3 parents felt uncomfortable sharing their spending because the scale and context are very different from teens.
InsightThis suggests that users need contextual guidance, not raw comparison.
Design responseOnly teens are sharing selected budgeting to parents or guardians.
Pillar 04 Detail
Safe AI for Young Users

Habitta's AI is designed to keep teens safe. It follows clear rules, and if it notices signs that someone may be at risk, it shows a helpline and alerts the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) — the trained person responsible for child safety.

What the AI never does in a crisis
  • End the conversation suddenly
  • Lecture or ask clinical questions
  • Generate its own crisis response
  • Assume the home is safe
What the AI always does instead
  • Stay warm and present
  • Show one fixed helpline number
  • Alert the DSL immediately
  • DSL decides on parent contact
