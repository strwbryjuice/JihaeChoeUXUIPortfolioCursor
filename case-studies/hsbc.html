Project Overview
Background
Bridging the gap between a user's perceived knowledge and their actual ability to detect a scam.

When users experience fraud, they often stop using HSBC entirely — meaning fraud must be prevented before it occurs, not just reimbursed after the fact.

Evidence-Based Intervention
Applying the right brakes at the right time.
Main Features
Feature 01
Smart Intervention
Automated warnings that trigger during the transfer flow if the recipient's account is high-risk (e.g., newly created, flagged, or from a high-scam region).
GIF
Feature 02
Behavioral Micro-tips
Visual cues that debunk specific scam tactics (like "Safe Account" scams) on the loading screen before a transaction is finalized.
GIF
Feature 03
Quick Cancel from App Notification
Allows users to cancel unauthorized transactions directly from the push notification without opening the app, minimizing the "Window of Opportunity" for scammers.

Feature 04
Encouraging Lowering Transaction Limit
Before the scam happens, we suggest the bank's scam prevention method in advance, instead of expecting the user to already know.

User Journey 1
Verifying suspicion came with too much friction

Scenario : User receives an SMS claiming suspicious activity or policy-related action try to check if this it is scam or not

User Journey Map
Initial Hypothesis 1
Verification becomes harder across fragmented touchpoints

When users try to verify a suspicious message, the information they need is spread across multiple touchpoints, including Google, the HSBC app, and the HSBC website. This fragmented experience increases cognitive load and makes users more vulnerable to mistakes under time pressure.

User Journey 2
When users are busy or distracted, the current system intervenes too late.

Scenario : User receives an SMS claiming to be from HSBC and lost money from putting personal info to scammer while User was busy to hang out with his friend

User Journey Map 2
Initial Hypothesis 2
Risk awareness may break down under pressure

Scam prevention currently depends too much on users recognizing risks on their own. But in urgent situations, people are less able to think clearly, making it difficult to rely on awareness alone. Users need more direct and timely support.

Hypothesis Wrap-up

Based on two user stories above again two hypothesis were declared, which get evaluate later in UTs.

Initial Hypothesis 2

User Story
Develop & Pivot: From Manual to Automated
  • What: Iterating on the "Instant Verification" concept.
  • How: Initially designed a manual "Message Verifier." However, UT1 revealed "Verification Inertia" — users felt checking a suspicious message was too much effort or unnecessary if they were already in a rush.
  • Why: Pivoted to Contextual Smart Overlays. Instead of asking the user to verify, the system automatically analyzes the recipient's data and provides "evidence-based friction."
Iteration / Pivot Diagram
Deliver
High-Fidelity Design
  • What: Final UI screens for the payment journey.
  • How: Designed a 3-layer risk detection UI:
    • Recency — Warning if the account is less than 3 months old.
    • Location — Warning if the account belongs to a high-risk region.
    • Social Proof — Warning if the account has multiple fraud reports.

Usability Testings
First Round · Concept Testing
Scam protection only works when it feels relevant
AimTo explore if all the functions are conceptually working in mid-fidelity stage
MethodThink aloud method and SUS · 45 minutes per person
Who5 HSBC account holders (4 did not get scammed, 1 got scammed)
Finding 01
Result User does not find the "Instant Verification" function necessary. They believe it is unnecessary action because they feel they already know what is a scam or not.
Insight We wonder if users know all the scenarios are fake because they haven't encountered a convincing one yet. Also start to think the first hypothesis could be wrong or not applicable.
Change Redesign the UT2 to be more urging — prompting users to verify suspicious activity instead of leaving it optional.
Finding 02
Result 3 out of 5 users questioned why they had to go through extra verification when they did not see themselves as suspicious.
Insight User expects scam prevention to focus more on the recipient than on the sender. So sender-focused verification feels unfair and unnecessary.
Change Added contextual warning popups about the recipient account when a transfer shows unusual or high-risk patterns.
UT1 Image
Second Round · Redesign Testing
More urgency, same rejection to verification function
AimTo evaluate whether a more urgent flow would encourage users to use the scam verification feature. Also to check if hypothesis 1 is right.
MethodThink aloud method and SUS · 45 minutes per person
Who5 HSBC account holders (all did not get scammed)
Finding 01
Result Even after redesigning UT2 to feel more urgent and time-sensitive, 2 out of 3 users still refused to use the Instant Verification feature. Users continued to see it as an unnecessary step because they believed they could judge scams on their own.
Insight The issue was not a lack of urgency, but a lack of perceived value. Users were overconfident in their ability to detect scams, so making the feature more urgent did not change their behavior.
Change Removed Instant Verification as a separate optional feature and instead integrated scam prevention cues directly into the money transfer flow.
UT2 Image
UT Reflection

Usability testing showed that the main issue was not simply the fragmented journey of verifying suspicious messages across multiple channels. More importantly, users were often unwilling to take extra steps to verify scams at all, even when urgency was increased. This revealed that the experience relied too heavily on users recognizing risk and acting on their own in moments of pressure.

Hypothesis 1 was invalidated
The fragmented journey itself was not the core issue.
Hypothesis 2 was validated
Under pressure, users' risk awareness broke down.
Final Design Direction
Instead of manual verification users could go through, give proactive warnings so users do not have to take extra steps but still remain safe.

Business Objective
Business Objective
Reduce reimbursement and potential customer loss

Bridging the gap between a user's perceived knowledge and their actual ability to detect a scam. The users experienced fraud decides to stop using HSBC, which means that in order to keep the user, fraud needs to be prevented in advance.

Conclusion
Take away

This process showed that test delivery matters: tone, urgency, gesture, and pacing should all be considered as part of the protocol. However, it also revealed that changing the delivery of a task does not necessarily change user behavior. Even interview-based hypotheses can be clearly rejected in usability testing, reinforcing the need for behavioral validation.

Limitation

While 30+ participants joined the survey, the study lacks diverse perspectives from multiple scam victims (only 1 interviewed). Users who experienced scams showed higher skepticism towards the researcher's approach. With more time and outreach, having 4 more scam victims' voices would make this study more mature.
