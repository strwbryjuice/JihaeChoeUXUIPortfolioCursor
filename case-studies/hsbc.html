HSBC Redesign for Cognitive Fraud Prevention

Role
Lead Product Designer
Timeline
Sep – Dec 2025 · 3 months

Project Overview
Background
Bridging the gap between a user's perceived knowledge and their actual ability to detect a scam.

When users experience fraud, they often stop using HSBC entirely — meaning fraud must be prevented before it occurs, not just reimbursed after the fact.

Background Image
Keywords
Evidence-Based Intervention
Applying the right brakes at the right time.
User Overconfidence
The biggest vulnerability in fraud prevention.
High Cognition Load
When user is busy on doing something the scam happens.
Main Features
Feature 01
Smart Intervention
Automated warnings that trigger during the transfer flow if the recipient's account is high-risk (e.g., newly created, flagged, or from a high-scam region).
GIF
Feature 02
Behavioral Micro-tips
Visual cues that debunk specific scam tactics (like "Safe Account" scams) on the loading screen before a transaction is finalized.
GIF
Feature 03
Quick Cancel from App Notification
Allows users to cancel unauthorized transactions directly from the push notification without opening the app, minimizing the "Window of Opportunity" for scammers.
GIF
Feature 04
Encouraging Lowering Transaction Limit
Before the scam happens, we suggest the bank's scam prevention method in advance, instead of expecting the user to already know.

Primary Research · Survey
Users are overconfident — but only until scam actually happens.
WhoUK bank users from Reddit and LinkedIn Community, age range 20 to 50s
How22 questions with Google Form, expected to spend 15 minutes
Num33 participants
PurposeTo check user awareness and behaviour around scam detection
Insight 01
Parallel to desk research, user overestimates their ability to recognize what banks will never ask for.
Evident 1. Around 60% of users think they know 2–4 rules they must not do against scam, which is relatively a lot to practice.
Graph
Evident 2. Around 60% of users think they can provide information to scammers that a bank will never ask from users.
Graph
Insight 02
Scam is not driven from lack of bank's scam explanation, but from absence of a real/fake detecting tool.
Evident 1. More than half of users say they understand why and what banks require.
Graph
Evident 2. However when roughly 70% of users face scam or scam-like messages they cannot tell if it is a scam.
Graph
Evident 3. Key anxiety triggers are "repeated verification, account lockouts, urgent wording, and complexity."

User Story
Develop & Pivot: From Manual to Automated
  • What: Iterating on the "Instant Verification" concept.
  • How: Initially designed a manual "Message Verifier." However, UT1 revealed "Verification Inertia" — users felt checking a suspicious message was too much effort or unnecessary if they were already in a rush.
  • Why: Pivoted to Contextual Smart Overlays. Instead of asking the user to verify, the system automatically analyzes the recipient's data and provides "evidence-based friction."
Iteration / Pivot Diagram
Deliver
High-Fidelity Design
  • What: Final UI screens for the payment journey.
  • How: Designed a 3-layer risk detection UI:
    • Recency — Warning if the account is less than 3 months old.
    • Location — Warning if the account belongs to a high-risk region.
    • Social Proof — Warning if the account has multiple fraud reports.

Develop · Usability Testing
First Round · Concept Testing
From Manual to Automated
  • What: Iterating on the "Instant Verification" concept.
  • Aim: Task base UT1 explores if any error or miscommunication happens in initial 5 ideas.
  • Insight 1: User does not find the "Instant Verification" function necessary. They believe it is unnecessary action because they feel they already know what is a scam or not.
    So — Removed "Instant Verification" function completely. Instead, focus on the money sending process to have extra cautious warnings at the right time.
  • Insight 2: User questions why the sender has to verify identity — they believe the receiver should be checked more instead.
UT1 Image
Second Round · Redesign Testing

Tested the Smart Overlays. Users reported a much higher "Sense of Security" because the app gave them specific reasons (the 3 layers of risk) rather than a generic "Be careful" message.

UT2 Image
Conclusion & Limitations
Key Learning

Great security design isn't about teaching the user; it's about the system doing the heavy lifting when the user is under cognitive load.

Limitation

While 30+ participants joined the survey, the study lacks diverse perspectives from multiple scam victims (only 1 interviewed). Users who experienced scams showed higher skepticism towards the researcher's approach. With more time and outreach, having 4 more scam victims' voices would make this study more mature.
