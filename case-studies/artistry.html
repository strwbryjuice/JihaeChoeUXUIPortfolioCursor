Project Overview

Artistry Community is an online exhibition platform for emerging artists. While the platform made artworks accessible online, the existing experience felt closer to a slideshow than an exhibition: viewers mainly clicked through pages linearly, and artists gained visibility but little tangible follow-up value.

This project aimed to redesign the exhibition flow so that it could feel more immersive for viewers and more meaningful for artists through stronger structure, richer interaction, and feedback pathways.

After the redesign, artist satisfaction increased by 20%, drop-off decreased by 20%, and average duration increased by 10% despite the page count being reduced from 30 to 20.

Observe
Artist Interviews
Exposure meant little unless it led to credibility or opportunity

Artist interviews revealed that visibility was the platform's clearest value, but also its biggest limitation. All 4 artists said exposure mattered, yet none described the exhibition as leading to strong tangible follow-up opportunities. What they wanted was not just to be seen, but to gain credibility, feedback, stronger context around their work, and clearer pathways to future connection.

Maze Survey · Viewers
The platform turned the exhibition into a passive, linear sequence of images rather than a curated exploration.

Maze testing showed that the viewer problem was not simply about visual preference. It was behavioral. 62% of participants wanted to revisit a specific work or section, and 33% of unfinished participants said the experience was not engaging enough. Heatmaps reinforced the same pattern across layouts: users kept clicking "next" instead of engaging deeply with the artwork. I also observed that drop-off peaked around page 20, when the exhibition was already about two-thirds complete and the content became more text-heavy. 3 out of 6 exits happened at that point, suggesting that repeated next-clicking followed by dense reading created fatigue and encouraged viewers to leave.

Define
The Problem

"The platform made artworks accessible, but functioned more as a content container rather than an immersive exhibition experience, limiting both viewer immersion and artist opportunity."

Hypothesis
H1 · Exhibition Structure
If the exhibition is reduced to fewer than 20 pages and the structure becomes less linear and more revisitable, viewers will feel less fatigue and be more likely to complete the exhibition.

Maze showed that 62% of viewers wanted to return to a specific artwork or section. I also observed that drop-off peaked around page 20, when the exhibition was already about two-thirds complete and became more text-heavy. This suggested that the long, forward-only flow was creating fatigue and did not support revisiting well.

H2 · Richer Interaction Through Artist Voice
If the exhibition moved beyond repeated next-clicking and gave viewers more contextual ways to engage with each artwork and the artist behind it, the experience would feel more engaging.

Maze showed weak engagement: 33% of participants who stopped said it was "not engaging enough," and 25% wanted bigger images, suggesting a need for closer looking and deeper engagement. Viewer comments described the experience as too static and portfolio-like, while all 4 artists emphasized the importance of artist voice and context.

H3 · Feedback Pathways
If artists could collect feedback and visible engagement signals, the platform would feel more valuable beyond exposure alone.

Maze showed weak post-viewing action signals: the average likelihood of leaving a reaction/comment was only 3.2/5, and the average likelihood of sharing the exhibition was also 3.2/5. In artist interviews, artists repeatedly said that exposure without feedback, contact, or follow-up opportunity did not feel especially valuable.

We initially had 20 possible ideas and later prioritised them through cross-background voting with design, engineering, and CEO stakeholders.

Developer Feedback
Designing for Content Safety and Easier Data Handling

For MVP 03 (Visitor Book), the developer raised a safety concern: placing an open text field directly on the exhibition page could attract bot spam, ads, or inappropriate content. To reduce this risk, we introduced an extra step before users could access the input area. We implemented this step through a Google Form rather than a custom-designed page, which also made data collection and handling easier for the development team.

Validation
H1 · Exhibition Structure
If the exhibition is reduced to fewer than 20 pages and the structure becomes less linear and more revisitable, viewers will feel less fatigue and be more likely to complete the exhibition.

This hypothesis would be supported if:

  • exits decreased on longer or text-heavy sections
  • users were less likely to leave because of confusion or unclear progress
  • completion-related behaviour improved in the revised flow
H2 · Richer Interaction Through Artist Voice
If the exhibition moved beyond repeated next-clicking and gave viewers more contextual ways to engage with each artwork and the artist behind it, the experience would feel more engaging.

This hypothesis would be supported if:

  • average duration increased even with fewer pages
  • users interacted with new features beyond simple next-clicking
  • drop-off decreased despite a shorter exhibition flow
H3 · Feedback Pathways
If artists could collect feedback and visible engagement signals, the platform would feel more valuable beyond exposure alone.

This hypothesis would be supported if:

  • artist satisfaction increased after the redesign
  • post-viewing action signals (reactions, shares) improved compared to baseline
